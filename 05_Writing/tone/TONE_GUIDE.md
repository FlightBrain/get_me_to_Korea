# Tone Guide

**Purpose:** Reference for voice, style, and approach across different writing contexts. Ensures consistency and helps you match tone to audience expectations.

**How to Use:** Before writing, identify your channel and audience. Review the relevant section. Write your draft. Read it against the dos/don'ts. Adjust until it feels right for that context.

---

## Cold Email Tone

### Core Principles
- **Specific, not broad.** You've researched this person. Show it.
- **Peer, not vendor.** You're a thoughtful person sharing an observation, not a salesperson delivering a pitch.
- **Conversational, not formal.** Short sentences. Active voice. Real language.
- **Curious, not pushy.** "I'm wondering..." > "You need..."

### Dos
- ✓ Use contractions (I'm, you're, we've)
- ✓ Mention something specific about their company/recent activity
- ✓ Ask a real question you want answered
- ✓ Be specific about next steps ("15 minutes next Tuesday?" vs. "let's connect")
- ✓ Acknowledge you're an inbound message ("I know this is an ask")
- ✓ Write like you talk (not like a press release)
- ✓ Keep openers to 2-3 sentences max
- ✓ Show you understand their problem before pitching your solution

### Don'ts
- ✗ Generic openers that apply to 100 people
- ✗ Fake compliments ("Your team is doing amazing things!")
- ✗ Corporate jargon ("synergistic solutions," "leveraging")
- ✗ Long paragraphs (break into 2-3 sentence chunks)
- ✗ Urgent language that isn't actually urgent
- ✗ "Reaching out" (just say what you want)
- ✗ Multiple CTAs (one clear ask)
- ✗ Overpromising ("This will change your business")

### Example Cold Email (Good)
"Noticed you just launched a multi-agent framework on your blog. I've been watching teams tackle agent eval infrastructure and wanted to surface one thing: the ones solving it early are shipping 3x faster by Series B. Curious how you're thinking about quality checks as complexity scales?"

### Example Cold Email (Bad)
"Hi, I hope this email finds you well. We're a leading platform in AI evaluation and observability. I think your company would be a great fit for our solution. Would love to chat about how we can help you leverage AI more effectively. Let me know what times work best for you!"

## LinkedIn Tone

### Core Principles
- **Authoritative but approachable.** You know your domain. Don't be arrogant about it.
- **Transparent about stakes.** Share real numbers, real challenges, real lessons.
- **Conversation starter, not lecture.** Invite engagement, don't just broadcast.
- **Occasionally vulnerable.** "We got this wrong..." builds more trust than "best practices are..."

### Dos
- ✓ Write sentences with enough weight and context to feel like real thoughts, not a highlight reel
- ✓ Combine related ideas into longer, flowing sentences when the meaning benefits
- ✓ Start with a hook (question, surprising stat, contrary take)
- ✓ Share specific numbers when possible
- ✓ Admit uncertainty or mistakes
- ✓ End with a genuine question or call to reflect
- ✓ Use paragraph breaks (not line breaks) for readability
- ✓ Use contractions to keep writing natural
- ✓ Tag relevant people sparingly (not everyone you mention)
- ✓ Respond to early comments within 1 hour
- ✓ Keep posts concise overall: fewer sentences that each carry more weight

### Don'ts
- ✗ Inspiration porn ("If you're reading this, you're a winner!")
- ✗ Performative vulnerability ("Honestly, I almost gave up...")
- ✗ Any hashtags, ever
- ✗ "It's not X, it's Y" or "It isn't just X, it's Y" constructions
- ✗ Short, punchy, fragmented sentences that read like a highlight reel
- ✗ Fishing for comments ("Drop a 🔥 if you agree!")
- ✗ Corporate speak
- ✗ Humble bragging ("Excited to announce I've been alive for 30 years!")
- ✗ Posting without proofreading
- ✗ Engagement bait unrelated to your actual expertise
- ✗ Links anywhere except as the final CTA at the bottom

### Example LinkedIn Post (Good)
"We shipped 47 AI features last year, and we caught quality issues on exactly three of them before customers reported them.

The other 44 had problems that slipped to production, ranging from minor latency spikes to expensive hallucinations that confused our users.

This year we're building eval infrastructure so that number comes from a system, not luck. Anyone else dealing with this, and how are you catching quality issues before production?"

### Example LinkedIn Post (Bad)
"Excited to announce we're revolutionizing the AI space! 🚀 Our platform is the best in the industry and our customers love us. If you're interested in joining the future of technology, check us out today! #AI #Innovation #StartupLife #Grateful #Blessed"

## X/Twitter Tone

### Core Principles
- **Hot takes are fine; bad faith isn't.** Controversial ≠ wrong. Be opinionated but fair.
- **Casual but smart.** Confident, conversational, clear without sounding stiff.
- **Permissive of casual language.** X is conversational. Email is professional.
- **Reply and engage.** It's a conversation, not a broadcast.

### Dos
- ✓ Lead with the hottest take (not the nuance)
- ✓ Use threads to expand on nuance
- ✓ Engage with others' posts (ratio: 1 original, 5 replies)
- ✓ Use line breaks for pacing and emphasis
- ✓ Retweet + add value (don't just retweet)
- ✓ Be playful, use casual language and contractions
- ✓ Make strong claims (and back them up in thread)
- ✓ Post when you have energy to engage
- ✓ Put links in a separate tweet within the thread, never inline

### Don'ts
- ✗ Vague post fishing for engagement
- ✗ Any hashtags, ever
- ✗ "It's not X, it's Y" or "It isn't just X, it's Y" constructions
- ✗ Dunking on people (mockery without intellectual substance)
- ✗ Very long single tweets (use threads)
- ✗ Posting politics/religion if not your domain
- ✗ Dunking on competitors (focus on your approach, not theirs)
- ✗ Replying when frustrated/tired
- ✗ Taking yourself too seriously
- ✗ Engagement bait

### Example X Post (Good)
"everyone talks about 'agentic AI' but very few teams have figured out how to actually measure if it works

the ones who did are shipping features 3x faster because they built measurement into the workflow from the start, not as an afterthought"

### Example X Post (Bad)
"check out our new AI evaluation platform! It's the best in the industry. Click the link to learn more. #AI #innovation"

## Internal Slack Tone

### Core Principles
- **Direct and informal.** This is internal—you can be casual and brief.
- **Async-friendly.** Write complete thoughts so people can respond without you present.
- **Action-oriented.** Clear owners, clear deadlines, clear next steps.
- **Low ego.** Questions and admissions of uncertainty are fine.

### Dos
- ✓ Use threads to keep channels clean
- ✓ Be direct about needs/problems
- ✓ Assume good intent
- ✓ Ask for feedback/thoughts
- ✓ Update people on progress, not just problems
- ✓ Keep it brief (Slack, not email)
- ✓ Use emojis for tone (👍 for acknowledgment, ⚠️ for blockers)

### Don'ts
- ✗ Formal language (save that for email)
- ✗ Venting without proposing solutions
- ✗ Passive-aggressive tone
- ✗ Going around someone (mention @them if it's about them)
- ✗ Assuming context (brief people quickly)
- ✗ Urgent flags for non-urgent things
- ✗ Typing a novel (break it into parts)

### Example Slack Message (Good)
"Hey team, got feedback from [Company] call yesterday—they need quicker turnaround on eval results. Current bottleneck is data processing step. I'm digging into optimizations, but wanted to flag this might affect our June roadmap. Will have options by EOD Thursday. Any context on this from your side?"

### Example Slack Message (Bad)
"URGENT!!! WE NEED TO TALK ABOUT THE EVAL PIPELINE NOW!!! This is a disaster and nobody is taking it seriously. I can't believe we shipped this like this. Everyone needs to drop what they're doing and fix it. This is THE BIGGEST PRIORITY."

## Follow-Up Tone

### Core Principles
- **Acknowledge the lack of response; don't blame them.** "Probably buried in your inbox..."
- **Add new value, not just re-ask.** Give them a reason to revisit.
- **Shorter than the original.** They didn't reply because it wasn't top-of-mind; don't make it longer.
- **Respectful of their time.** If they didn't respond, maybe it's not a fit. Offer an exit.

### Dos
- ✓ Reference your original message briefly
- ✓ Add new insight or evidence
- ✓ Offer an escape hatch ("Or if timing isn't right, I get it")
- ✓ Shorten the CTA (was 15 min, now "or just a quick call?")
- ✓ Space follow-ups 1-2 weeks apart
- ✓ Limit to 2-3 follow-ups max

### Don'ts
- ✗ Guilt ("I haven't heard from you...")
- ✗ Multiple follow-ups in same week
- ✗ Longer follow-ups than original
- ✗ Repeating the same message verbatim
- ✗ Frustrated tone
- ✗ More than 3 follow-ups total
- ✗ Following up on "no thanks"

### Example Follow-Up (Good)
"Quick follow-up on my note about eval infrastructure—probably buried in your inbox, which I totally get.

Just wanted to resurface: most teams I talk to hit this problem around Series B when manual testing stops scaling. Knowing your growth trajectory, thought it might be timely.

Either way, let me know if a conversation makes sense. Otherwise, all good."

### Example Follow-Up (Bad)
"Hi, I noticed you didn't respond to my previous email. I'm very interested in speaking with you about our platform. This is a critical opportunity for your company and I think you're making a mistake by not responding. Please get back to me ASAP. This is urgent."

## Kensington's Approved Outbound Email Formula

This is the specific structure for 1-off outbound emails (not sequences):

1. **Their recent AI/GenAI release or signal** (shows homework)
2. **1-line relevant case study** (matched to their industry/role, see SELF_PROMPT Section 4)
3. **Link to braintrust.dev/customers** (let the page sell, not the email)
4. **Discovery question about pain point** (how they're doing evals, biggest challenge, what's broken)
5. **"Best," + HTML signature** (see USER_PREFERENCES.md)

**Rules:**
- Every email must be uniquely worded. No two emails should read the same, even within the same account.
- Subject lines: internal/casual, not salesy. Lowercase. Problem-first.
- Under 100 words body.
- No em dashes ever.
- The disco question is the CTA, not "want to chat?"
- Always use `contentType: "text/html"` with the HTML signature.
