# Messaging Principles

## Purpose
Define universal rules for all outreach messaging—email, LinkedIn, X, comments. These principles ensure consistency, credibility, and higher engagement across all channels.

## How to Use
Before sending any outreach, check your draft against these six principles. Revise until it passes all six tests. Use Braintrust angles as shortcuts for injecting credibility.

---

## The Six Rules

### **1. Lead with Insight, Not Pitch**
Spend 70% on context/insight, 30% on CTA. The prospect should finish your message thinking "that's interesting" not "oh, another sales email."

**Bad:** "Braintrust helps teams build better AI evals. Want to see a demo?"

**Good:** "Saw you shipped Claude integration in v0.3. Multi-model agents make evals harder—most teams are still doing it manually. Quick thought: the best signal often comes from user feedback loops, not just test cases."

**Why it works:** You've referenced specific knowledge, named a real problem, and planted a question in their head—not asking for anything yet.

---

### **2. Reference Specific Signals**
Every outreach must cite 1–2 real signals. Generic is dead.

**Signals to use:**
- Company hired VP of ML / Head of AI in past 6 months
- Shipped new AI product feature or announced AI-powered update
- Raised funding (Series A, B, venture round)
- Technical blog post or whitepaper on LLMs, agents, evals
- GitHub repo showing agent-related code (Claude SDK, LangChain, etc.)
- X/LinkedIn post from CTO or VP Eng about shipping or testing challenges
- Job posting for AI/ML or "prompt engineer" roles
- Conference talk or podcast appearance on AI topic

**Bad:** "I noticed you work in AI"

**Good:** "Saw the announcement about your Claude-powered workflow automation layer—evals get exponentially harder when agents interact with external APIs."

**Why it works:** Signals prove you've done homework. No homework = spam.

---

### **3. Use Their Language**
Mirror the terminology and tone of your prospect. If they say "evaluation," don't say "testing." If they're technical, don't dumb it down.

**Signal sources:**
- Recent job postings (vocabulary for what they value)
- Blog/tech posts (how they explain problems)
- X/LinkedIn posts (formal or casual tone)
- Company website messaging (vision, positioning)

**Bad (too formal for a startup CTO):** "We would appreciate the opportunity to demonstrate our enterprise-grade evaluation orchestration platform."

**Good (matching startup tone):** "We make it easy to iterate on agent behavior without the spreadsheet madness."

**Why it works:** Language matching builds rapport. Mismatched tone signals you don't understand their world.

---

### **4. Keep It Short**
Cold email should be 50–100 words. LinkedIn message: 3–4 sentences. X engagement: One hook + one insight.

**Bad (180 words):** Long paragraph explaining what Braintrust does, company history, why evals matter, case studies...

**Good (80 words):** "Saw you hired for two AI engineer roles last month. The hard part isn't building the agent—it's knowing if it's better after your changes. Most teams test in production or Excel. What's your current workflow?"

**Why it works:** Busy people respect brevity. Longer messages feel like you're trying to convince them before they even know if they're interested.

---

### **5. Always Have a Clear CTA**
Every message needs one ask. Not two, not "let me know," not "interested?" Be specific.

**Bad CTAs:** "Let me know if you'd like to connect," "Interested in learning more?" "Happy to chat anytime!"

**Good CTAs:**
- "Quick question: how are you currently testing agent behavior?"
- "Want to see how [similar company] cuts eval time from weeks to hours?"
- "Worth a 15-min call to see if we can help?"
- "Can I send over a one-pager on how this works?"

**Why it works:** Specific asks get higher response rates. Vague ones get ignored or soft-rejected.

---

### **6. Avoid Corporate Speak**
No jargon clouds, no motherhood statements, no buzzwords. Be direct and human.

**Bad:** "Leveraging advanced evaluation frameworks to unlock agentic potential through systematic measurement paradigms."

**Good:** "You're already thinking about evals. We make that faster."

**Bad:** "Synergize your observability stack with cutting-edge AI orchestration solutions."

**Good:** "See what's happening inside your agent without the spreadsheet overhead."

**Why it works:** Real engineers are skeptical of hype. Clarity = credibility.

## REAL OUTREACH PATTERNS THAT WORK

### Pattern 1: Dave/Dexcom Formula (Financial Reports + 2-3 Bullets)
**When to use:** Prospect has clear public AI narrative (press release, earnings call, product launch)
**Formula:**
1. Pull signal from financial report/press/news ("Saw you launched medical device with AI diagnostics")
2. Map to their specific AI product/pain (e.g., "medical devices need accuracy every time")
3. Drop 2-3 bullets on what success looks like (e.g., "90.2% accuracy on test set," "audit trail for FDA")
4. NO CTA — just information
5. **Result:** "why don't you just send me an order form?" response rate is common

**Example:** Email to medical device CEO: "Saw your Q2 earnings mention the new AI diagnostic feature. Medical devices need different eval standards than consumer apps—every 0.1% accuracy matters. Similar situation at Dexcom (Dave's company). Worth a conversation?"

**Key:** No ask, just signal + pattern recognition + social proof

---

### Pattern 2: Manish/Brian Multi-Thread Formula (Public Anchor + Gap + Logos)
**When to use:** Multiple decision-makers at same company; need to break through with multiple angles
**Formula:**
1. Anchor to something public they said (LinkedIn post, blog, talk, job posting)
2. Name the specific gap ("You're hiring eval engineers but haven't built the infrastructure they'd use")
3. Drop 2 company logos who hit the same wall (Notion, Zapier, Dropbox examples)
4. Close with braintrust.dev/customers (no ask, just FYI)
5. **Result:** CFO, VP Eng, and CEO all see the same signal from different angles

**Example (Threaded emails):**
- Email 1 (to VP Eng): "Saw your job posting for 'AI Quality Engineer.' That role usually reports they're rebuilding eval infrastructure. Notion solved it differently. [link]"
- Email 2 (to CTO): "Hired a bunch of ML engineers? Here's how Zapier scaled quality alongside engineering velocity. [link]"
- Email 3 (to CEO via LinkedIn): "Growth inflection often means quality risk if eval infrastructure isn't there. Worth a quick thought."

**Key:** Same message, multiple entry points, no CTA, just pattern recognition

---

### Pattern 3: Ghost Notes (Executive-to-Executive Outreach)
**When to use:** You want to open doors for SDRs; have executive credibility; want to reach C-level
**Formula:**
1. Bryan/Drew (executives) write short, executive-level note (150 words max)
2. From their name directly (not SDR)
3. Acknowledge their specific AI challenge (not generic)
4. Offer value (case study, framework, intro) without asking for call
5. **Result:** C-level reads it, often responds, forwards to team member ("Have an SDR follow up")

**Example:** From VP Product at Braintrust to Fortune 500 Chief AI Officer: "Saw your announcement about AI governance initiative. Getting 70 engineers aligned on eval standards is hard—we did it with Notion. Quick framework if useful. [link]"

**Key:** Executive voice, executive problem, value-first, SDR follow-up later

---

### Pattern 4: Event-Driven Outreach (HumanX, Dinners, Warriors Games)
**When to use:** Major event (HumanX conference, Braintrust dinners, sports games, industry conferences)
**Formula:**
1. Primary hook is the event itself (social proof, FOMO, organic conversation starter)
2. Secondary hook: "Director can't get into VIP event" angle (we're offering access)
3. Close with "1:1 to discuss what you're working on" (not a pitch)
4. **Result:** 3-5x higher response rate; conversation happens organically at event, not via email

**Example (HumanX conference):** "I noticed you're interested in attending HumanX in May. We have an extra VIP pass and think it'd be valuable to connect with you beforehand. What's the biggest AI evaluation challenge you're tackling this quarter?"

**Key:** Event is the real value prop; 1:1 is secondary; high engagement rate

---

### Pattern 5: Pigment Playbook (Conference → Ghost Notes → Internal Connections → Close)
**When to use:** Major account; CEO/founder at industry conference; high-profile target
**Formula:**
1. See CEO at conference (e.g., Founder Summit, Scale Conf)
2. Immediately post-conference: Write 3 ghost notes (CEO, VP Eng, CFO) from Braintrust execs
3. Identify internal connections (Trey knows someone on their board, etc.)
4. Hit from multiple angles simultaneously (ghost notes + warm intro + event follow-up)
5. **Result:** 1-2 week turnaround from "spotted at conference" to qualified call

**Example timeline:**
- Day 1: See Pigment CEO at Founder Summit
- Day 1 (afternoon): 3 ghost notes sent from Braintrust leadership
- Day 2: Warm intro from Trey (board member connection)
- Day 3: VP Product calls, lands meeting
- Week 2: Close call, deal progresses

**Key:** Concentration of signals; multiple channels; executive voices; internal leverage

## RED LINES (Never Do This)

- ❌ Don't include info dumps (long paragraph about features)
- ❌ Don't use em dashes — use periods. (Cleaner, crisper voice)
- ❌ Don't claim Braintrust "solves" AI—we're infrastructure
- ❌ Don't shame their current process ("you're probably using Excel" feels gross)
- ❌ Don't use "just checking in" or "following up" without new value
- ❌ Don't make it about us; make it about their problem
- ❌ Don't use ALL CAPS or multiple punctuation marks
- ❌ Don't mention Navan for non-voice problems
- ❌ Don't pitch before they've responded to your first message
- ❌ Don't use corporate jargon ("leverage," "synergize," "paradigm shift")

---

## TESTING YOUR MESSAGE

Before sending any outreach:
1. Would I respond to this if I received it?
2. Does it prove I've done research (specific signal)?
3. Is there a specific ask at the end (or intentional no-ask)?
4. Could I remove 30 words without losing meaning?
5. Is the language theirs, not ours?
6. Did I avoid em dashes and corporate speak?
7. Does it anchor to a real signal (not generic)?

If you answer no to any of these, revise before sending.
